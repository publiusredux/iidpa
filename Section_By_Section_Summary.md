# Section-by-Section Summary
### Immigration Integrity and Due Process Act (IIDPA)
Prepared by **Publius Redux**

---

# **Section 1 — Short Title; Purpose; Findings; Definitions**

**What it does:**
- Establishes the Act's short title
- States the Act's purposes: restore lawful order, protect constitutional rights, strengthen labor enforcement, create compliance pathways, and impose fair consequences
- Provides congressional findings emphasizing the need for a structured, rights-respecting reform framework
- Defines key terms including *unlawful presence*, *minor civil violation*, *Strike*, *Conditional Residency*, *Worker Compliance Visa*, *Wind-Down WCV*, *stateless person*, *manufactured statelessness*, *unaccompanied minor*, *aging-out*, *existing deferred action recipient*, *common control*, *Good Faith Compliance Certificate*, *sectoral staggering*, *approved third-party auditor*, and *extreme humanitarian case*

**Why it matters:**
Creates the legal foundation and ensures consistent interpretation across DHS, USCIS, DOL, DOJ, and related agencies. The expanded definition set reduces interpretive fragmentation across field offices and adjudicative bodies.

---

# **Section 2 — Enforcement Limitations**

**What it does:**
- Requires reasonable articulable suspicion of a violation of federal immigration law or other criminal offense before detaining or requesting identification — explicitly scoped to interior enforcement
- Preserves existing border checkpoint and border zone authority under 8 U.S.C. §1357(a)(3)
- Prohibits enforcement based solely on race, ethnicity, accent, language, perceived immigration status, or appearance; clarifies these factors may be considered as part of a totality-of-circumstances analysis but do not individually constitute reasonable suspicion
- Establishes an evidentiary exclusion rule for egregious or bad-faith violations
- Requires DHS to adopt strict data-minimization standards under 6 U.S.C. §1523(b)
- Establishes agency-level civil monetary penalties for substantiated patterns of pretextual stops; authorizes suspension of federal grant funding under 34 U.S.C. §10151 et seq. for systemic violations; preserves individual officer good-faith carve-out

**Why it matters:**
Protects constitutional rights, prevents profiling, standardizes lawful enforcement procedures, and creates a financial deterrent for jurisdictions that engage in de facto profiling through pretextual stops without creating individual officer liability complications.

---

# **Section 3 — Disqualifying Offenses**

**What it does:**
Enumerates serious offenses that bar eligibility for all pathways, including:
- Aggravated felonies (INA §101(a)(43))
- Crimes of violence (18 U.S.C. §16)
- Felony crimes against children
- Sex-offender registration violations
- Drug trafficking (21 U.S.C. §§841, 846)
- Terrorism-related offenses (INA §212(a)(3))

**Why it matters:**
Protects public safety by ensuring that individuals with serious criminal histories cannot access compliance pathways.

---

# **Section 3A — Asylum and Humanitarian Protections**

**What it does:**
- Explicitly preserves asylum eligibility under INA §208, withholding of removal under INA §241(b)(3), Convention Against Torture protections, and non-refoulement obligations under the 1967 Refugee Protocol
- Establishes Strike tolling during the pendency of bona fide asylum claims, with reinstatement upon denial
- Prohibits use of Door 1 as a mechanism to remove individuals with pending or viable asylum claims
- Provides that individuals with disqualifying offenses remain subject to expedited removal except where removal would violate non-refoulement obligations, in which case withholding of removal applies

**Why it matters:**
Ensures the bill's enforcement mechanisms do not conflict with binding international law obligations or existing domestic asylum protections. Non-refoulement is a jus cogens norm — this section prevents the bill from being challenged or struck down on those grounds.

---

# **Section 4 — Compliance Pathways ("Four Doors")**

Creates four structured options for individuals to comply with immigration law, depart voluntarily, or transition into lawful status. Includes transition provisions for existing deferred action recipients, stateless persons, and unaccompanied minors before the four pathway doors.

---

## **Section 4.0 — Transition Provisions for Existing Deferred Action Recipients**

**What it does:**
- Grandfathers existing DACA and deferred action recipients into Door 2 eligibility without restarting the continuous residence clock
- Treats DACA-eligible individuals who never applied or whose applications lapsed as standard Door 2 applicants
- Preserves disqualifying offense ineligibility regardless of deferred action status

**Why it matters:**
Addresses approximately 580,000 active DACA recipients and prevents the bill from being silent on a major existing population, closing a significant litigation and political vulnerability.

---

## **Section 4.1 — Stateless Persons**

**What it does:**
- Routes stateless persons to Doors 2, 3, or 4 where Door 1 departure cannot be effected due to lack of receiving country
- Establishes a USCIS statelessness determination process within 180 days of enactment using UNHCR evidentiary standards
- Excludes manufactured statelessness — voluntary renunciation or fraudulent claims — from protection
- Carves out persecution-based denationalization: state-imposed denationalization resulting from ethnic persecution, religious persecution, political retaliation, or crimes against humanity does not constitute manufactured statelessness
- Provides supervised release consistent with *Zadvydas v. Davis* where removal cannot be effected

**Why it matters:**
Closes a legal dead end for a small but genuine population and prevents a loophole by which individuals could manufacture statelessness to avoid removal, while protecting those who had no agency in losing nationality.

---

## **Section 4.2 — Unaccompanied Minors**

**What it does:**
- Defers to TVPRA and applicable settlement agreements for unaccompanied minors; Strikes do not apply to minors
- Provides a 180-day aging-out transition window upon reaching age 18; prior unlawful presence as a minor does not count against eligibility
- Carves out serious juvenile offenses — homicide, rape or sexual assault, aggravated assault causing serious bodily injury, and terrorism-related conduct — from the minor exemption upon aging out
- Requires mandatory individualized hearing before applying permanent ineligibility based on juvenile conduct, consistent with *Miller v. Alabama* and *Roper v. Simmons*
- Minor offenses including vandalism, petty theft, and minor drug possession do not carry forward

**Why it matters:**
Prevents the bill from conflicting with existing TVPRA and *Flores* settlement frameworks while closing the obvious exploit that the minor exemption could shield serious violent offenders.

---

## **Door 1 — Enhanced Voluntary Departure**

**What it does:**
- Provides a one-time, biometric-verified voluntary departure option with no removal order and no Strike
- Election constitutes a knowing and voluntary waiver of INA §240 proceedings, administered in applicant's primary language with access to counsel
- Election constitutes waiver of defensive asylum claims based on pre-departure circumstances; post-departure claims based on materially new evidence of persecution preserved
- Eligible electors placed in priority queue for H-2A and Door 3 WCV nonimmigrant visa categories for future lawful return
- Wind-Down WCV holders departing within 60-day window upon staggering expiration receive equal priority queue access
- Unavailable to individuals with pending or viable asylum claims or to stateless persons where removal cannot be effected

**Why it matters:**
Creates an orderly, administratively clean exit mechanism that is more rational than underground status, without creating a litigation entry point through last-minute defensive asylum filings.

---

## **Door 2 — Residency Track**

**What it does:**
- Requires 4 years continuous residence (up to 8 years with one minor civil violation)
- Requires tax compliance, English proficiency or active enrollment, biometric enrollment, and annual reporting
- Leads to Conditional Residency, Lawful Permanent Residency, and potential citizenship
- Strike 1 imposes a 5-year bar; disqualifying offenses terminate eligibility
- Uncontested applications processed through IIDPA Adjudication Office non-adversarial track, not EOIR

**Why it matters:**
Creates an earned, structured pathway without blanket amnesty. Administrative off-ramp prevents compliance volume from overwhelming immigration courts.

---

## **Door 3 — Labor Integrity Track**

**What it does:**
- Phases in nationwide E-Verify over 5 years beginning with a pilot in 5 states
- Anti-fragmentation clause: entities under common control per 26 U.S.C. §414(b)-(c) treated as single employer for 50-employee threshold; 24-month lookback with rebuttable presumption of evasion for prior restructuring
- Safe Harbor: employers completing DOL-approved third-party Integrity Audit within first 12 months receive Good Faith Compliance Certificate waiving fragmentation presumption for prior restructuring; audit costs tax-deductible
- Tiered civil penalties: $1,000 / $5,000 / $10,000 (systemic) / criminal (willful systemic)
- Worker Compliance Visa: 3-year renewable visa with one standard renewal; mandatory year-4 Door 2 transition assessment; third renewal option contingent on demonstrated Door 2 progress; supervised departure option for holders unable to qualify
- WCV data not usable for enforcement except for fraud, national security, or disqualifying offenses
- Uncontested applications processed through IIDPA Adjudication Office non-adversarial track

**Why it matters:**
Improves labor enforcement, closes the shell-company evasion loophole, protects workers, expands lawful workforce participation, and creates a structured employer compliance framework with both deterrence and good-faith incentives.

---

## **Door 3 — Labor Bridge Provision**

**What it does:**
- Strike 1 recipients employed at E-Verify compliant firms eligible for 90-day conversion to Door 3 WCV status in lieu of immediate departure
- Employer must be actively E-Verify compliant at time of Strike; worker must have no additional violations
- Conversion counts as standard WCV renewal; 5-year Door 2 bar remains fully in effect

**Why it matters:**
Prevents acute supply chain disruption in labor-dependent sectors by providing an orderly transition mechanism without eliminating Strike consequences.

---

## **Door 4 — National Service Track**

**What it does:**
- Allows eligible individuals to earn status through 4 years of military or civilian national service
- 2,000 military slots and 5,000 civilian slots annually
- Leads to Conditional Residency, LPR status, and fast-track naturalization
- Status revocable for misconduct or fraud

**Why it matters:**
Rewards meaningful service while contributing to national workforce and security needs.

---

# **Section 5 — Two-Strike System**

**What it does:**
- **Strike 1:** First post-enactment violation triggers 5-year bar from Doors 2-4; eligible for Door 1 or INA §240 proceedings; Labor Bridge conversion available for qualifying employed individuals
- **Strike 2:** Second violation permanently bars eligibility for all Doors and legal re-entry; mandatory individualized hearing required before permanent bar applies; waiver only for extreme humanitarian cases
- Strikes tolled during pending asylum claims; reinstated upon denial
- Strikes do not apply to unaccompanied minors
- **Sectoral Staggering Authority (5.4):** DHS may authorize phased departures in a specific NAICS sector upon joint USDA/DOL certification of projected 15% labor shortfall; 180-day increments with re-certification requirement; 24-month hard cap with no exceptions; Wind-Down WCV issued to affected workers; 60-day departure window upon expiration with automatic Strike 2 for non-compliance

**Why it matters:**
Creates a predictable, deterrence-based enforcement structure with built-in economic safety valves that prevent enforcement from triggering acute supply chain failures.

---

# **Section 6 — Retroactivity and Transition**

**What it does:**
- Applies Strikes only to conduct occurring after enactment
- Establishes a 36-month transition period tied to EOIR capacity benchmarks including minimum staffing levels, per-judge caseload targets, and processing time standards
- Transition period extends automatically in 6-month increments if benchmarks are not met
- Missed benchmarks trigger mandatory supplemental appropriation of up to $500 million; corrective action plan required within 30 days; plan eligible for committee discharge after 60 days

**Why it matters:**
Ensures fairness and operational readiness before full enforcement applies, and prevents the compliance window from closing before courts have the capacity to handle the resulting caseload.

---

# **Section 7 — Sunset and Long-Term Operation**

**What it does:**
- Sunsets Door 1 after 15 years
- Makes Doors 2-4 permanent
- Retains the Strike system indefinitely
- Provides a 6-month grace period for pending Door 1 applicants after sunset

**Why it matters:**
Transitions the system to long-term stability and lawful compliance pathways.

---

# **Section 8 — Oversight, Appeals, and Agency Penalties**

**What it does:**
- Establishes the Immigration Integrity Inspector General with annual reporting on systemic violations, enforcement fairness, labor integrity, border metrics, Door utilization, and pretextual stop pattern findings
- Mandatory periodic GAO audits with enforceable corrective deadlines
- 30-day appeals window for Strikes, disqualifications, or pathway denials
- Appeals heard by EOIR in first instance; BIA on further appeal; appropriate U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals consistent with INA §242
- De novo review of legal determinations; substantial evidence standard for factual findings
- 90-day EOIR decision mandate with automatic BIA review upon failure
- Up to 10% funding reduction for agencies with systemic violations; administrative compensation for affected individuals

**Why it matters:**
Ensures accountability, transparency, uniform enforcement, and a specified appellate structure that prevents forum ambiguity in litigation.

---

# **Section 9 — Fiscal Provisions**

**What it does:**
- Caps total spending at $15-$30 billion over 10 years
- Funds programs through penalties, fees, DHS/DOL reallocations, and capped appropriations
- Establishes mandatory baseline appropriation floor of $1.5 billion per fiscal year for 7 years, inflation-adjusted; explicitly prohibits offset, rescission, or reprogramming based on penalty revenue fluctuations
- Dedicated EOIR court expansion appropriation for surge hiring of immigration judges, asylum officers, and administrative law judges
- IIDPA Adjudication Office within USCIS for non-adversarial processing of uncontested Door 2 and Door 3 applications
- Door 1 surge capacity funding for biometric enrollment, departure logistics, and receiving country coordination
- Secretary of State directed to negotiate Expedited Return Protocols with high-volume sending nations within 18 months; cooperating nations eligible for reintegration assistance from penalty revenues
- Interim implementation budget trigger: core components funded from penalty revenues pending CBO score; appropriation-dependent components sunset if appropriations not enacted within 24 months
- CBO score required within 120 days of enactment

**Why it matters:**
Maintains fiscal responsibility, prevents the system from defunding itself as enforcement succeeds, and ensures court and administrative capacity funding is protected from political reprogramming.

---

# **Section 10 — Severability**

**What it does:**
Ensures the remainder of the Act remains valid if any individual provision is struck down.

---

# **Section 11 — Effective Date**

**What it does:**
Makes the Act effective 180 days after enactment; DHS and USCIS must issue implementing regulations within the same 180-day period.

---

# **Section 12 — Border Security & Humanitarian Provisions**

**What it does:**
- Allocates $5 billion for border sensors, drones, aerial surveillance, Border Patrol hiring and retention, and integrated data systems
- Provides case-by-case family unity and medical hardship waivers for Doors 2-4
- Requires annual public reporting on border encounter volume, immigration court backlogs, and unlawful-presence population estimates

**Why it matters:**
Improves border capacity while incorporating humane safeguards and maintaining public accountability through annual reporting.

---

# **Section 13 — Rule of Construction**

**What it does:**
- Clarifies the Act does not create new legal rights beyond existing constitutional protections
- Preserves rights guaranteed by the Fourth Amendment, Fifth Amendment, and INA §240
- Requires DHS and USCIS to publish implementing regulations within 180 days consistent with the Administrative Procedure Act

**Why it matters:**
Prevents misinterpretation and ensures timely execution consistent with existing administrative law requirements.

---

# **Section 14 — Definitions Governance and Interpretive Authority**

**What it does:**
- Establishes a tri-layer interpretive authority structure: DHS (operational definitions), USCIS (benefits eligibility), DOJ/EOIR (adjudicatory interpretation)
- Conflict resolution hierarchy: DHS governs unless dispute relates solely to adjudicatory procedure
- Skidmore-weight deference for agency interpretations issued through notice-and-comment rulemaking
- Requires notice-and-comment rulemaking with 60-day public comment period for any definitional modification; prohibits informal guidance or policy memoranda from modifying statutory definitions
- Requires consistent interpretation of definitions across all implementing agencies
- Preserves INA §242 judicial review standards; where direct conflict arises between agency interpretive authority and INA §242, standards and procedures of INA §242 control

**Why it matters:**
Prevents interpretive fragmentation across agencies and field offices, reduces litigation over definitional ambiguity, and locks definitions against administrative drift between administrations without requiring congressional action for every clarification.

---

# **Section 15 — Implementation Authority**

**What it does:**
- Emergency hiring authority under 5 U.S.C. §§2103 and 3302: expedited hiring for immigration judges, asylum officers, USCIS adjudicators, and IT security personnel; term-limited appointments; reemployed annuitants; OPM priority hiring track within 90 days
- IT procurement authority: streamlined acquisition up to $150 million per contract action; interagency contracting authority; national security system designation; NIST SP 800-53 cybersecurity governance framework within 180 days
- Fund transfer and reprogramming authority: 15% cap per appropriation account per fiscal year; 30-day advance notification to Appropriations Committees for actions exceeding $10 million; authority expires at 7-year mark
- IIDPA Program Management Office established within DHS within 90 days: administrative and operational authority only, no adjudicatory authority; interoperability certification authority; quarterly implementation reports to Congress; PMO certification required before any data system component declared operational

**Why it matters:**
Converts the bill from policy architecture into an executable federal program by unlocking the hiring, procurement, and coordination mechanisms necessary to stand up the system within the 180-day effective date window. Without this section, normal federal procurement and hiring timelines would make the bill's implementation deadlines functionally impossible.

---

# **Section 16 — Federal Preemption**

**What it does:**
- Express field preemption of civil immigration enforcement procedures, compliance pathways, and immigration status determination systems governed by this Act
- Preempts state immigration-status-based detention triggers inconsistent with Section 2 standards; state employer verification systems inconsistent with E-Verify upon phase-in; state immigration status databases inconsistent with DHS integrated data system upon certification
- Savings clause: preserves state criminal law enforcement unrelated to immigration status, cooperation consistent with DHS protocols, general applicability state laws, and consistent state employer conduct laws
- Data system exclusivity: states may not maintain immigration status databases inconsistent with or duplicative of DHS integrated data system; unauthorized integration constitutes basis for suspension of federal grant funding

**Why it matters:**
Prevents 50-state enforcement divergence, forecloses parallel state immigration systems that would fragment the integrated data architecture, and provides a legally clean federal supremacy framework while preserving legitimate state enforcement cooperation through the savings clause.
