# Executive Summary
### Immigration Integrity and Due Process Act (IIDPA)
Prepared by **Publius Redux**

---

## Overview and Purpose

The Immigration Integrity and Due Process Act (IIDPA) is a comprehensive immigration reform proposal addressing the estimated **11–14 million** individuals residing in the United States without lawful status. The Act establishes an orderly and constitutionally grounded framework for **compliance, departure, or enforcement**, while protecting civil liberties, enhancing national security, strengthening labor markets, and ensuring due process.

Rooted in Congress's plenary authority and aligned with the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), IIDPA avoids both mass amnesty and mass removal. Instead, it provides **four structured compliance pathways**, a **two-strike deterrence system**, and **rigorous oversight** designed to restore public trust and long-term stability.

The v2.0 revision substantially expands the bill's legal architecture, adding asylum and humanitarian protections, transition provisions for existing deferred action recipients, stateless person and unaccompanied minor frameworks, labor market safety valves, and a full governance architecture covering interpretive authority, implementation machinery, and federal preemption.

---

## Key Findings and Necessity

Congress identifies that:

- Prolonged widespread unlawful presence places significant strain on border enforcement, immigration courts, detention systems, and labor markets
- Existing statutory frameworks provide no clear, scalable mechanisms for long-term compliance
- Unlawful presence fosters exploitation, inconsistent enforcement, and public distrust
- A structured, rights-respecting system is needed to restore order and constitutional integrity

IIDPA establishes a framework designed to reduce unlawful presence by an estimated **20–40% over 10 years**, measured through DHS's Estimated Unauthorized Resident Population (EURP) reports (based on 2023 baseline estimates ranging from 11–14 million across sources including DHS, Pew Research, MPI, and CMS).

## Core Provisions

### Enforcement Limitations (Section 2)

- Requires **reasonable articulable suspicion of a violation of federal immigration law or other criminal offense** before detention or identification requests — explicitly scoped to interior enforcement
- Preserves existing border checkpoint and border zone authority
- Prohibits enforcement based solely on race, ethnicity, language, accent, or perceived immigration status; clarifies totality-of-circumstances analysis remains available
- Establishes **agency-level civil monetary penalties** for substantiated patterns of pretextual stops, with federal grant funding suspension for systemic violations
- Incorporates Supreme Court limitations on admissible evidence

### Disqualifying Offenses (Section 3)

Individuals convicted of serious public safety offenses — including aggravated felonies (INA §101(a)(43)), crimes of violence (18 U.S.C. §16), felony crimes against children, sex-offender registration violations, drug trafficking (21 U.S.C. §§841, 846), and terrorism-related activities (INA §212(a)(3)) — are ineligible for all compliance pathways.

### Asylum and Humanitarian Protections (Section 3A)

- Explicitly preserves asylum eligibility under INA §208, withholding of removal under INA §241(b)(3), and non-refoulement obligations under the 1967 Refugee Protocol and Convention Against Torture
- Strikes are tolled during pending asylum claims and reinstated upon denial
- Door 1 unavailable to individuals with pending or viable asylum claims

---

## Transition Provisions

### Existing Deferred Action Recipients (Section 4.0)
Active DACA and deferred action recipients are grandfathered into Door 2 eligibility without restarting the continuous residence clock. Disqualifying offenses remain applicable.

### Stateless Persons (Section 4.1)
Stateless persons are routed to Doors 2, 3, or 4 where departure cannot be effected. A USCIS statelessness determination process is established using UNHCR evidentiary standards. Manufactured statelessness is excluded from protection; persecution-based denationalization is explicitly carved out.

### Unaccompanied Minors (Section 4.2)
Governed by TVPRA and applicable settlement agreements. Strikes do not apply to minors. A 180-day aging-out transition applies upon reaching age 18. Serious juvenile offenses — homicide, rape or sexual assault, aggravated assault causing serious bodily injury, and terrorism-related conduct — trigger ineligibility upon aging out, subject to mandatory individualized hearing.

## Compliance Pathways ("Four Doors")

### Door 1 — Enhanced Voluntary Departure
- Biometric-verified departure; penalty waivers; one-time use; no Strike applied
- Election constitutes knowing and voluntary waiver of INA §240 proceedings and pre-departure asylum claims
- Eligible electors placed in **Priority Queue** for H-2A and WCV nonimmigrant visa categories for future lawful return

### Door 2 — Residency Track
- 4–8 years continuous residence, tax compliance, English proficiency
- Leads to Conditional Residency → LPR → citizenship eligibility
- Uncontested applications processed through non-adversarial USCIS administrative track

### Door 3 — Labor Integrity Track
- 5-year nationwide E-Verify phase-in with anti-fragmentation provisions under 26 U.S.C. §414(b)-(c)
- Safe Harbor: DOL-approved third-party Integrity Audit within first 12 months provides Good Faith Compliance Certificate
- Tiered employer penalties: $1,000 / $5,000 / $10,000 (systemic) / criminal (willful systemic)
- Renewable 3-year **Worker Compliance Visa** (WCV) with year-4 Door 2 transition assessment and third renewal option
- **Labor Bridge:** Strike 1 recipients at E-Verify compliant firms eligible for 90-day conversion to WCV status; 5-year Door 2 bar preserved

### Door 4 — National Service Track
- 4 years of military or civilian national service (2,000 military / 5,000 civilian annual slots)
- Leads to Conditional Residency and fast-track naturalization

## Two-Strike System

| Strike | Trigger | Consequences |
|--------|---------|--------------|
| **Strike 1** | First post-enactment unlawful entry or overstay | 5-year bar from Doors 2-4; Door 1 or INA §240 proceedings; Labor Bridge available for qualifying employed individuals |
| **Strike 2** | Second post-enactment violation | Permanent ineligibility for all Doors; permanent re-entry bar; mandatory individualized hearing; narrow humanitarian waiver |

Strikes tolled during pending asylum claims. Strikes do not apply to unaccompanied minors.

### Sectoral Staggering Authority (Section 5.4)
Where joint USDA/DOL certification confirms a projected 15% labor shortfall in a specific NAICS sector, DHS may authorize phased departures in 180-day increments up to a 24-month hard cap. Wind-Down WCVs issued to affected workers; Door 1 Priority Queue available upon clean departure within 60-day expiration window.

---

## Sunset Mechanism

- Door 1 expires after **15 years**
- Doors 2-4 and the Strike system continue permanently
- 6-month grace period for pending Door 1 applicants at sunset

---

## Oversight and Appeals (Section 8)

- Independent **Immigration Integrity Inspector General** with annual reporting on systemic violations, enforcement fairness, Door utilization, and pretextual stop patterns
- Mandatory periodic GAO audits with enforceable corrective deadlines
- **30-day appeals window** for denials and Strike assessments
- Appeals: EOIR in first instance → BIA → appropriate U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals consistent with INA §242
- De novo review of legal determinations; substantial evidence standard for factual findings
- 90-day EOIR decision mandate; automatic BIA review upon failure
- Up to 10% funding reduction for agencies with systemic violations

---

## Border Security & Humanitarian Measures (Section 12)

- **$5 billion** for sensors, drones, aerial surveillance, integrated data systems, and Border Patrol hiring and retention
- Family unity and medical hardship waivers for Doors 2-4
- Annual public reporting on encounter volume, court backlogs, and unlawful-presence trends

---

## Fiscal Framework (Section 9)

- Funded through penalties, fees, reallocations, and capped appropriations of **$15-$30 billion over 10 years**
- **Mandatory baseline appropriation floor** of $1.5 billion per fiscal year for 7 years, inflation-adjusted; explicitly non-reclassifiable based on penalty revenue fluctuations
- Dedicated EOIR court expansion appropriation for surge hiring of immigration judges and asylum officers
- IIDPA Adjudication Office within USCIS for non-adversarial processing of uncontested pathway applications
- Secretary of State directed to negotiate Expedited Return Protocols with high-volume sending nations within 18 months
- CBO score required within 120 days of enactment

---

## Governance Architecture (Sections 14-16)

### Definitions Governance (Section 14)
Tri-layer interpretive authority — DHS (operational), USCIS (benefits), EOIR (adjudicatory) — with Skidmore-weight deference, notice-and-comment requirements for definitional changes, and INA §242 judicial review preservation.

### Implementation Authority (Section 15)
Emergency hiring authority, streamlined IT procurement up to $150 million per contract action, fund transfer and reprogramming authority, and a centralized **IIDPA Program Management Office** within DHS with administrative and operational authority only — no adjudicatory authority.

### Federal Preemption (Section 16)
Express field preemption of civil immigration enforcement procedures and compliance systems; conditional preemption of conflicting state verification systems and databases; savings clause preserving state criminal enforcement and cooperation; data system exclusivity with federal grant funding enforcement lever.

---

## Implementation and Safeguards

- Takes effect **180 days** after enactment
- 36-month transition period tied to EOIR capacity benchmarks; extends automatically if benchmarks unmet
- Strikes apply prospectively only
- Strict data-minimization standards under 6 U.S.C. §1523(b)
- Fraud penalties under INA §212(a)(6)(C)
- Severability clause preserves statute if individual provisions are struck

---

## Expected Impacts

IIDPA:

- Strengthens labor integrity and reduces unauthorized hiring through E-Verify phase-in and anti-fragmentation enforcement
- Enhances national security vetting through biometric enrollment and integrated data systems
- Provides structured, constitutional, non-amnesty compliance options for long-term residents
- Prevents supply chain disruption through Sectoral Staggering Authority
- Protects due process at every stage while targeting serious offenders for removal
- Channels long-term residents into lawful, economically productive pathways
- Establishes a governance architecture capable of surviving administration changes and litigation challenges
