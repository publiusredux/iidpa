IMMIGRATION INTEGRITY AND DUE PROCESS ACT (IIDPA)
A Bill
To establish an orderly immigration compliance framework; deter repeated violations; protect constitutional rights; strengthen labor enforcement; and reduce long-term unlawful presence while respecting the rule of law.

SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE; PURPOSE; FINDINGS; DEFINITIONS
1.1 Short Title
This Act may be cited as the "Immigration Integrity and Due Process Act" or "IIDPA."
---
1.2 Purpose
The purpose of this Act is to:
Establish a lawful and orderly immigration compliance system;
Protect individual rights under the Constitution;
Enhance national security and public safety;
Strengthen labor integrity and reduce unlawful employment;
Provide clear pathways for compliance; and
Apply firm, fair consequences for repeated unlawful entry or overstays.
The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) shall administer and enforce this Act consistent with the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), including §103.
---
1.3 Findings
Congress finds that:
Several million individuals currently reside in the United States without lawful status, contributing to strain on border enforcement, immigration courts, detention capacity, and labor markets;
Existing statutes lack clear, workable mechanisms for large-scale compliance;
Prolonged unlawful presence fosters exploitation, inconsistent enforcement, and diminished public trust;
Mass amnesty and mass removal are neither feasible nor consistent with constitutional values;
A structured, rights-respecting statutory framework is necessary to restore order and uphold constitutional protections;
Congress possesses plenary authority over immigration.
---
1.4 Constitutional Enforcement Standards
All persons within the U.S. are protected by constitutional guarantees, including the Fourth Amendment;
No officer may detain, question, or compel identification absent objective, articulable reasonable suspicion of a specific criminal offense;
Race, ethnicity, national origin, language, accent, appearance alone, or perceived immigration status do not constitute reasonable suspicion;
Enforcement must follow constitutional precedent;
Nothing herein creates a new cause of action beyond existing constitutional law.
---
1.5 Definitions
The following terms are defined for clarity and consistency with the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA) and constitutional requirements:
Status-Related Terms
Aging-out: The transition of an unaccompanied minor to adult status upon reaching age 18, triggering a 180-day window to apply for an appropriate Door under this Act.
Conditional Residency (CR): Temporary lawful status under Doors 2 or 4.
Compliance window: The 180-day period designated by DHS for pathway applications.
Enhanced voluntary departure: Voluntary departure consistent with INA §240B, with biometric verification and penalty waivers.
Existing deferred action recipient: Any individual holding active DACA status or analogous deferred action status as of the date of enactment, as documented by USCIS records.
Manufactured statelessness: Statelessness resulting from voluntary renunciation of citizenship, fraudulent misrepresentation of nationality, or other self-induced acts undertaken for the purpose of circumventing removal or obtaining immigration benefits under this Act.
Minor civil immigration violation: Overstay or lapse under 180 days without fraud or criminal conduct.
Stateless person: An individual who is not considered a national by any state under the operation of its law, consistent with Article 1 of the 1954 Convention Relating to the Status of Stateless Persons.
Unaccompanied minor: A child who has no lawful immigration status in the United States, has not attained 18 years of age, and who has been separated from both parents or is being sought to be admitted to the United States and whose entry is sought without a parent or legal guardian, consistent with 6 U.S.C. §279(g)(2).
Unlawful presence: Presence without admission or parole as described in INA §212(a)(9)(B).
Worker Compliance Visa (WCV): Temporary, renewable nonimmigrant classification under Door 3.
Process & Oversight Terms
Due process: Rights guaranteed under the Fifth Amendment and INA §240, including notice, hearing, ability to present evidence, and right to counsel at personal expense.
Extreme humanitarian case: Hardship involving U.S.-citizen dependents, life-threatening medical conditions, or similar circumstances.
Fraudulent application: Conduct described in INA §212(a)(6)(C).
Strike: A civil penalty applied under Section 5.
Substantiated complaint: A complaint verified by the Office of Inspector General or an authorized investigative body.
Systemic violation: Three or more confirmed violations of Section 2 within a 12-month period.
Qualification Requirements
English proficiency: Basic comprehension of ordinary English for naturalization purposes under INA §312, with hardship waivers where applicable.
Small employer: As defined under 26 U.S.C. §414(b)-(c).
Service & Program Terms
Alternative civilian service: AmeriCorps or similar service authorized under 42 U.S.C. §12501 et seq.
Clarifying Definitions
The following terms are further clarified with references to existing statutes and case law for operational precision:
Enhanced surveillance: Use of fixed towers, autonomous sensors, UAVs, and mobile surveillance systems consistent with 8 U.S.C. §1103 note.
Integrated data system: Cross-agency DHS–DOL–DOJ platform using privacy-preserving protocols under 6 U.S.C. §1523(b), as approved by the Office of Management and Budget.
Integrity audit: A compliance review under amended INA §274A(d)(4), including document verification, payroll analysis, and anomaly detection.
Reasonable suspicion standard: Consistent with constitutional limits and Supreme Court precedent, including Arizona v. United States, 567 U.S. 387 (2012).

SECTION 2. ENFORCEMENT LIMITATIONS
2.1 Requirements for Stops and Identification
No officer may detain, stop, or compel identification absent reasonable suspicion of a specific criminal offense.
Immigration status alone is insufficient.
This does not modify border-checkpoint authority under 8 U.S.C. §1357(a)(3).
---
2.2 Prohibited Bases for Enforcement
Officers may not use:
Race
Ethnicity
National origin
Perceived immigration status
Language or accent
Appearance alone
---
2.3 Evidentiary Exclusion
Evidence obtained through egregious or bad-faith violations of this Section is inadmissible in civil immigration proceedings, consistent with INS v. Lopez-Mendoza (1984).
---
2.4 Data-Privacy Standards
DHS must implement data-minimization consistent with 6 U.S.C. §1523(b).
---
SECTION 3. DISQUALIFYING OFFENSES
Individuals convicted of any of the following are ineligible for all Doors and may face expedited removal:
Aggravated felonies (INA §101(a)(43));
Crimes of violence (18 U.S.C. §16);
Felony crimes against children;
Sex-offender registration offenses;
Drug trafficking (21 U.S.C. §§841, 846);
Terrorism-related offenses (INA §212(a)(3)).
---
SECTION 3A. ASYLUM AND HUMANITARIAN PROTECTIONS — PRESERVATION OF RIGHTS
3A.1 Preservation of Asylum Rights
Nothing in this Act modifies, limits, or supersedes:
Asylum eligibility under INA §208;
Withholding of removal under INA §241(b)(3);
Protection under the Convention Against Torture, consistent with 8 C.F.R. §208.16–208.18;
Non-refoulement obligations binding on the United States under the 1967 Protocol Relating to the Status of Refugees and Article 3 of the United Nations Convention Against Torture.
3A.2 Strike Tolling During Pending Asylum Claims
No Strike shall accrue during the pendency of a bona fide asylum claim filed under INA §208;
Removal may not be executed while an asylum claim is pending adjudication or on appeal;
Upon denial of an asylum claim, any previously tolled Strike shall be reinstated and applied consistent with Section 5;
Voluntary departure under Door 1 shall not be used as a mechanism to remove any individual with a pending or viable asylum claim.
3A.3 Interaction with Disqualifying Offenses
Individuals with disqualifying offenses under Section 3 remain subject to expedited removal notwithstanding asylum claims, except where removal would violate non-refoulement obligations under §3A.1;
In such cases, withholding of removal under INA §241(b)(3) shall apply in lieu of full asylum consideration.

SECTION 4. COMPLIANCE PATHWAYS ("FOUR DOORS")
---
SECTION 4.0 — TRANSITION PROVISIONS FOR EXISTING DEFERRED ACTION RECIPIENTS
4.0.1 DACA and Deferred Action Eligibility
Existing deferred action recipients as defined in Section 1.5 are eligible for Door 2 without restarting the continuous residence clock;
Time accrued under DACA or analogous deferred action status counts toward the 4-year continuous residence requirement under Section 4.5;
Existing deferred action status does not constitute a Strike and shall not be counted against an applicant under Section 5.
4.0.2 DACA-Eligible Individuals Who Did Not Apply
Individuals who were eligible for DACA but never applied, or whose applications lapsed, are eligible for Door 2 on the same terms as any other applicant;
No special grandfather treatment applies; standard Door 2 eligibility requirements under Section 4.5 govern.
4.0.3 Disqualifying Offenses
Existing deferred action recipients with disqualifying offenses under Section 3 remain ineligible for all Doors. Deferred action status confers no exemption from Section 3.
---
SECTION 4.1 — STATELESS PERSONS
4.1.1 Routing of Stateless Persons
Stateless persons as defined in Section 1.5 are ineligible for Door 1 (Enhanced Voluntary Departure) where no country of nationality will issue travel documents or accept return;
Stateless persons shall be routed to Doors 2, 3, or 4 based on individual circumstances and eligibility requirements.
4.1.2 Statelessness Determination
USCIS shall establish a statelessness determination process within 180 days of enactment;
Determination shall follow UNHCR statelessness determination procedures or equivalent evidentiary standards;
Burden of proof rests on the applicant to affirmatively demonstrate statelessness by a preponderance of the evidence.
4.1.3 Manufactured Statelessness Exclusion
Manufactured statelessness as defined in Section 1.5 does not qualify for stateless person protections under this Section;
Voluntary renunciation of citizenship is a disqualifying basis for stateless person protections;
False claims of statelessness constitute fraudulent application under INA §212(a)(6)(C);
Statelessness occurring within 5 years of application triggers heightened scrutiny for manufactured statelessness, including cross-referencing of foreign nationality records through the integrated data system;
Only individuals who had no meaningful agency in becoming stateless qualify for protections under this Section.
4.1.4 Zadvydas Compliance
Consistent with Zadvydas v. Davis, 533 U.S. 678 (2001), no stateless person may be held in detention where removal is not reasonably foreseeable;
Where removal cannot be effected within a reasonable period due to statelessness, supervised release with reporting requirements shall apply in lieu of continued detention.
---
SECTION 4.2 — UNACCOMPANIED MINORS
4.2.1 Governing Framework
Unaccompanied minors as defined in Section 1.5 are governed by the Trafficking Victims Protection Reauthorization Act (TVPRA) and applicable settlement agreements, not by IIDPA pathways or enforcement mechanisms;
Strikes under Section 5 do not apply to unaccompanied minors.
4.2.2 Aging-Out Transition
Upon reaching age 18, an unaccompanied minor shall have 180 days to apply for an appropriate Door based on individual circumstances;
Prior unlawful presence accrued as a minor shall not count against an aging-out applicant for Door eligibility purposes;
The aging-out transition period applies regardless of the minor's prior immigration proceedings.
4.2.3 Serious Juvenile Offense Carve-Out
Notwithstanding Section 4.2.1 and 4.2.2, an individual who was adjudicated delinquent as a minor for conduct that would constitute a disqualifying offense under Section 3 if committed by an adult is ineligible for all Doors upon aging out;
Disqualifying juvenile conduct includes homicide, rape or sexual assault, aggravated assault causing serious bodily injury, and terrorism-related conduct;
Minor juvenile offenses — including vandalism, petty theft, and minor drug possession — do not trigger ineligibility and do not carry forward into aging-out assessment;
A mandatory individualized hearing is required before applying permanent ineligibility based on juvenile conduct, consistent with Miller v. Alabama, 567 U.S. 460 (2012), and Roper v. Simmons, 543 U.S. 551 (2005);
A narrow rehabilitation exception is available at the individualized hearing, subject to a high evidentiary burden.
---
DOOR 1 — Enhanced Voluntary Departure
4.3 Eligibility
Any noncitizen without a disqualifying offense may elect enhanced voluntary departure, subject to the limitations of Sections 3A and 4.1.
4.4 Requirements
Biometric enrollment;
Identity verification;
Departure arrangements;
One-time use.
4.5 Outcomes
Departure without a removal order;
Penalty waiver;
No Strike applied;
Eligible for future lawful entry.
4.6 Limitations
Only medical-emergency waivers permitted;
Appeals limited to administrative error;
Door 1 is unavailable to individuals with pending or viable asylum claims under Section 3A;
Door 1 is unavailable to stateless persons where removal cannot be effected under Section 4.1.
---
DOOR 2 — Residency Track
4.7 Eligibility
4 years continuous residence;
Up to 8 years for one minor civil violation;
Tax compliance or payment plan;
No criminal convictions beyond civil violations;
English proficiency or instruction;
Biometric enrollment;
Annual reporting.
4.8 Outcomes
Conditional Residency;
LPR eligibility after completion;
Eligibility for citizenship per INA.
4.9 Limitations
Strike 1 imposes a 5-year bar.
Any disqualifying offense terminates eligibility.
---
DOOR 3 — Labor Integrity Track
4.10 E-Verify National Phase-In
Amends INA §274A:
5-year national phase-in;
Pilot in 5 states;
Small-employer exemption (<50 employees);
Funding per Section 9.
4.11 Employer Penalties and Incentives
Tax credits for voluntary disclosure;
Civil penalties: $1,000 → $5,000 → $10,000 (systemic);
Criminal penalties for willful systemic violations;
Whistleblower protections.
4.12 Worker Compliance Visa
Identity verification;
English instruction;
Tax compliance;
3-year renewable visa (one standard renewal);
WCV-to-Door 2 Transition Assessment: At year 4 of WCV status, USCIS shall conduct a mandatory assessment of the holder's eligibility for Door 2, providing a 2-year runway to cure deficiencies before the standard renewal period expires;
Third Renewal Option: A third renewal of up to 3 years is available contingent on demonstrated Door 2 progress, including active enrollment in English instruction, a tax compliance plan in place, and no new violations;
Supervised Departure Option: WCV holders who cannot meet Door 2 standards after the third renewal period are eligible for supervised departure under Door 1 terms, including penalty waivers, rather than accruing unlawful presence;
Strike Interaction: A WCV holder who receives a Strike 1 shall have a defined adjudication pathway and shall not automatically lose WCV status pending that adjudication;
Eligibility for Door 2 thereafter.
WCV information is not usable for enforcement except for fraud, national security, or disqualifying offenses.
---
DOOR 4 — National Service Track
4.13 Eligibility
No disqualifying offenses;
English proficiency;
Conditional pre-service status under amended 10 U.S.C. §504;
2,000 military slots/year;
5,000 civilian service slots/year.
4.14 Requirements
4 years active duty or civilian service;
4 years inactive reserve (military);
Good standing.
4.15 Outcomes
Conditional Residency;
LPR;
Fast-track naturalization;
Revocable for misconduct or fraud.

SECTION 5. TWO-STRIKE SYSTEM
5.1 Strike 1
Triggered by first post-enactment unlawful entry/overstay.
Consequences:
Eligibility for voluntary departure or INA §240 proceedings;
No removal order without INA §240 adjudication;
5-year bar from Doors 2–4;
Appeal rights.
5.2 Strike 2
Triggered by second violation.
Consequences:
Permanent ineligibility for all Doors;
Permanent re-entry bar;
Full due-process hearing;
Waiver only for extreme humanitarian cases.
5.3 Asylum Interaction
Strikes are tolled during the pendency of bona fide asylum claims consistent with Section 3A.2. Strikes do not apply to unaccompanied minors consistent with Section 4.2.1.
---
SECTION 6. RETROACTIVITY AND TRANSITION
Strikes apply only to post-enactment conduct.
36-month transition period funded per Section 9.
Capacity Benchmarking: The 36-month transition period is tied to immigration court capacity benchmarks established by the Executive Office for Immigration Review (EOIR) within 90 days of enactment. If court staffing and caseload capacity benchmarks are not met, the transition period shall extend automatically in 6-month increments until benchmarks are satisfied, preventing a compliance cliff absent adjudicative capacity.
---
SECTION 7. SUNSET AND LONG-TERM OPERATION
After 15 years:
Door 1 expires;
Doors 2–4 become permanent;
Strike system continues.
A 6-month grace period applies for pending Door 1 applicants.
---
SECTION 8. OVERSIGHT, APPEALS, AND AGENCY PENALTIES
8.1 Immigration Integrity Inspector General
Annual reports shall include:
Systemic violations;
Enforcement fairness;
Border and labor metrics.
8.2 Appeals
30-day appeals window for strikes, disqualifications, or denials;
Appeals Forum: Appeals shall be heard by the Executive Office for Immigration Review (EOIR) immigration courts in the first instance, with further appeal to the Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA) and thereafter to the appropriate U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals consistent with INA §242;
Standard of Review: De novo review of legal determinations; substantial evidence standard for factual findings;
Timeline: EOIR shall issue a decision within 90 days of a timely-filed appeal; failure to meet this deadline entitles the applicant to automatic administrative review by the BIA.
8.3 Penalties
Mandatory corrective action;
Up to 10% funding reduction for systemic violations;
Administrative compensation for affected individuals.
---
SECTION 9. FISCAL PROVISIONS
Funded through penalties, fees, DHS/DOL reallocations, and capped appropriations not exceeding $15–$30B over 10 years;
Fee schedules via regulation;
CBO score required within 120 days;
No unlimited mandatory spending;
Immigration Court Funding: A dedicated appropriation for immigration court expansion is established within the EOIR budget, separate from DHS/DOL reallocations, calibrated to projected caseload generated by Four Doors intake. This appropriation shall fund surge hiring of immigration judges, asylum officers, and administrative law judges, with a staffing needs assessment required within 90 days of enactment;
Interim Implementation Budget Trigger: Core operational components — biometric enrollment, compliance window administration, and court surge staffing — are funded on a priority basis from penalty revenues and existing reallocations pending CBO score completion. Components dependent on new appropriations shall sunset automatically if appropriations are not enacted within 24 months;
IIDPA Adjudication Office: A dedicated IIDPA adjudication office shall be established within USCIS to process Four Doors pathway applications, offloading volume from immigration courts during the transition period.
