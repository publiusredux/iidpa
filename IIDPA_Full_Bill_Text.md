# Immigration Integrity and Due Process Act (IIDPA)

> **A Bill** to establish an orderly immigration compliance framework; deter repeated violations; protect constitutional rights; strengthen labor enforcement; and reduce long-term unlawful presence while respecting the rule of law.

## Section 1. Short Title; Purpose; Findings; Definitions

### 1.1 Short Title

This Act may be cited as the **"Immigration Integrity and Due Process Act"** or **"IIDPA."**

---

### 1.2 Purpose

The purpose of this Act is to:

- Establish a lawful and orderly immigration compliance system
- Protect individual rights under the Constitution
- Enhance national security and public safety
- Strengthen labor integrity and reduce unlawful employment
- Provide clear pathways for compliance
- Apply firm, fair consequences for repeated unlawful entry or overstays

The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) shall administer and enforce this Act consistent with the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), including §103.

---

### 1.3 Findings

Congress finds that:

1. Several million individuals currently reside in the United States without lawful status, contributing to strain on border enforcement, immigration courts, detention capacity, and labor markets
2. Existing statutes lack clear, workable mechanisms for large-scale compliance
3. Prolonged unlawful presence fosters exploitation, inconsistent enforcement, and diminished public trust
4. Mass amnesty and mass removal are neither feasible nor consistent with constitutional values
5. A structured, rights-respecting statutory framework is necessary to restore order and uphold constitutional protections
6. Congress possesses plenary authority over immigration

---

### 1.4 Constitutional Enforcement Standards

- All persons within the U.S. are protected by constitutional guarantees, including the Fourth Amendment
- No officer may detain, question, or compel identification absent **objective, articulable reasonable suspicion** of a specific criminal offense
- Race, ethnicity, national origin, language, accent, appearance alone, or perceived immigration status do **not** constitute reasonable suspicion
- Enforcement must follow constitutional precedent
- Nothing herein creates a new cause of action beyond existing constitutional law

---

### 1.5 Definitions

#### Status-Related Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Aging-out** | The transition of an unaccompanied minor to adult status upon reaching age 18, triggering a 180-day window to apply for an appropriate Door under this Act |
| **Conditional Residency (CR)** | Temporary lawful status under Doors 2 or 4 |
| **Compliance window** | The 180-day period designated by DHS for pathway applications |
| **Enhanced voluntary departure** | Voluntary departure consistent with INA §240B, with biometric verification and penalty waivers |
| **Existing deferred action recipient** | Any individual holding active DACA status or analogous deferred action status as of the date of enactment, as documented by USCIS records |
| **Manufactured statelessness** | Statelessness resulting from voluntary renunciation of citizenship, fraudulent misrepresentation of nationality, or other self-induced acts undertaken for the purpose of circumventing removal or obtaining immigration benefits under this Act |
| **Minor civil immigration violation** | Overstay or lapse under 180 days without fraud or criminal conduct |
| **Stateless person** | An individual who is not considered a national by any state under the operation of its law, consistent with Article 1 of the 1954 Convention Relating to the Status of Stateless Persons |
| **Unaccompanied minor** | A child who has no lawful immigration status in the United States, has not attained 18 years of age, and who has been separated from both parents or is being sought to be admitted to the United States without a parent or legal guardian, consistent with 6 U.S.C. §279(g)(2) |
| **Unlawful presence** | Presence without admission or parole as described in INA §212(a)(9)(B) |
| **Worker Compliance Visa (WCV)** | Temporary, renewable nonimmigrant classification under Door 3 |

#### Process & Oversight Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Due process** | Rights guaranteed under the Fifth Amendment and INA §240, including notice, hearing, ability to present evidence, and right to counsel at personal expense |
| **Extreme humanitarian case** | Hardship involving U.S.-citizen dependents, life-threatening medical conditions, or similar circumstances |
| **Fraudulent application** | Conduct described in INA §212(a)(6)(C) |
| **Strike** | A civil penalty applied under Section 5 |
| **Substantiated complaint** | A complaint verified by the Office of Inspector General or an authorized investigative body |
| **Systemic violation** | Three or more confirmed violations of Section 2 within a 12-month period |

#### Qualification Requirements

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **English proficiency** | Basic comprehension of ordinary English for naturalization purposes under INA §312, with hardship waivers where applicable |
| **Small employer** | As defined under 26 U.S.C. §414(b)-(c), with aggregation of entities under common control |

#### Service & Program Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Alternative civilian service** | AmeriCorps or similar service authorized under 42 U.S.C. §12501 et seq. |

#### Clarifying Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Enhanced surveillance** | Use of fixed towers, autonomous sensors, UAVs, and mobile surveillance systems consistent with 8 U.S.C. §1103 note |
| **Integrated data system** | Cross-agency DHS-DOL-DOJ platform using privacy-preserving protocols under 6 U.S.C. §1523(b), as approved by the Office of Management and Budget |
| **Integrity audit** | A compliance review under amended INA §274A(d)(4), including document verification, payroll analysis, and anomaly detection, conducted by a DOL-approved third-party auditor |
| **Reasonable suspicion standard** | Consistent with constitutional limits and Supreme Court precedent, including *Arizona v. United States*, 567 U.S. 387 (2012) |

## Section 2. Enforcement Limitations

### 2.1 Requirements for Stops and Identification

- No officer may detain, stop, or compel identification absent reasonable suspicion of a specific criminal offense
- Immigration status alone is insufficient
- This does **not** modify border-checkpoint authority under 8 U.S.C. §1357(a)(3)

---

### 2.2 Prohibited Bases for Enforcement

Officers may not use any of the following as a basis for enforcement action:

- Race
- Ethnicity
- National origin
- Perceived immigration status
- Language or accent
- Appearance alone

---

### 2.3 Evidentiary Exclusion

Evidence obtained through egregious or bad-faith violations of this Section is inadmissible in civil immigration proceedings, consistent with *INS v. Lopez-Mendoza* (1984).

---

### 2.4 Data-Privacy Standards

DHS must implement data-minimization consistent with 6 U.S.C. §1523(b).

---

## Section 3. Disqualifying Offenses

Individuals convicted of any of the following are ineligible for all Doors and may face expedited removal:

- Aggravated felonies (INA §101(a)(43))
- Crimes of violence (18 U.S.C. §16)
- Felony crimes against children
- Sex-offender registration offenses
- Drug trafficking (21 U.S.C. §§841, 846)
- Terrorism-related offenses (INA §212(a)(3))

---

## Section 3A. Asylum and Humanitarian Protections — Preservation of Rights

### 3A.1 Preservation of Asylum Rights

Nothing in this Act modifies, limits, or supersedes:

- Asylum eligibility under INA §208
- Withholding of removal under INA §241(b)(3)
- Protection under the Convention Against Torture, consistent with 8 C.F.R. §208.16-208.18
- Non-refoulement obligations binding on the United States under the 1967 Protocol Relating to the Status of Refugees and Article 3 of the United Nations Convention Against Torture

---

### 3A.2 Strike Tolling During Pending Asylum Claims

- No Strike shall accrue during the pendency of a bona fide asylum claim filed under INA §208
- Removal may not be executed while an asylum claim is pending adjudication or on appeal
- Upon denial of an asylum claim, any previously tolled Strike shall be reinstated and applied consistent with Section 5
- Voluntary departure under Door 1 shall not be used as a mechanism to remove any individual with a pending or viable asylum claim

---

### 3A.3 Interaction with Disqualifying Offenses

- Individuals with disqualifying offenses under Section 3 remain subject to expedited removal notwithstanding asylum claims, except where removal would violate non-refoulement obligations under Section 3A.1
- In such cases, withholding of removal under INA §241(b)(3) shall apply in lieu of full asylum consideration

---

## Section 4. Compliance Pathways ("Four Doors")

---

### Section 4.0 — Transition Provisions for Existing Deferred Action Recipients

#### 4.0.1 DACA and Deferred Action Eligibility

- Existing deferred action recipients as defined in Section 1.5 are eligible for Door 2 without restarting the continuous residence clock
- Time accrued under DACA or analogous deferred action status counts toward the 4-year continuous residence requirement under Section 4.7
- Existing deferred action status does not constitute a Strike and shall not be counted against an applicant under Section 5

#### 4.0.2 DACA-Eligible Individuals Who Did Not Apply

- Individuals who were eligible for DACA but never applied, or whose applications lapsed, are eligible for Door 2 on the same terms as any other applicant
- No special grandfather treatment applies; standard Door 2 eligibility requirements under Section 4.7 govern

#### 4.0.3 Disqualifying Offenses

Existing deferred action recipients with disqualifying offenses under Section 3 remain ineligible for all Doors. Deferred action status confers no exemption from Section 3.

---

### Section 4.1 — Stateless Persons

#### 4.1.1 Routing of Stateless Persons

- Stateless persons as defined in Section 1.5 are ineligible for Door 1 (Enhanced Voluntary Departure) where no country of nationality will issue travel documents or accept return
- Stateless persons shall be routed to Doors 2, 3, or 4 based on individual circumstances and eligibility requirements

#### 4.1.2 Statelessness Determination

- USCIS shall establish a statelessness determination process within 180 days of enactment
- Determination shall follow UNHCR statelessness determination procedures or equivalent evidentiary standards
- Burden of proof rests on the applicant to affirmatively demonstrate statelessness by a preponderance of the evidence

#### 4.1.3 Manufactured Statelessness Exclusion

- Manufactured statelessness as defined in Section 1.5 does not qualify for stateless person protections under this Section
- Voluntary renunciation of citizenship is a disqualifying basis for stateless person protections
- False claims of statelessness constitute fraudulent application under INA §212(a)(6)(C)
- Statelessness occurring within 5 years of application triggers heightened scrutiny for manufactured statelessness, including cross-referencing of foreign nationality records through the integrated data system
- Only individuals who had no meaningful agency in becoming stateless qualify for protections under this Section

#### 4.1.4 Zadvydas Compliance

- Consistent with *Zadvydas v. Davis*, 533 U.S. 678 (2001), no stateless person may be held in detention where removal is not reasonably foreseeable
- Where removal cannot be effected within a reasonable period due to statelessness, supervised release with reporting requirements shall apply in lieu of continued detention

---

### Section 4.2 — Unaccompanied Minors

#### 4.2.1 Governing Framework

- Unaccompanied minors as defined in Section 1.5 are governed by the Trafficking Victims Protection Reauthorization Act (TVPRA) and applicable settlement agreements, not by IIDPA pathways or enforcement mechanisms
- Strikes under Section 5 do not apply to unaccompanied minors

#### 4.2.2 Aging-Out Transition

- Upon reaching age 18, an unaccompanied minor shall have 180 days to apply for an appropriate Door based on individual circumstances
- Prior unlawful presence accrued as a minor shall not count against an aging-out applicant for Door eligibility purposes
- The aging-out transition period applies regardless of the minor's prior immigration proceedings

#### 4.2.3 Serious Juvenile Offense Carve-Out

Notwithstanding Sections 4.2.1 and 4.2.2, an individual adjudicated delinquent as a minor for conduct that would constitute a disqualifying offense under Section 3 if committed by an adult is ineligible for all Doors upon aging out.

Disqualifying juvenile conduct includes:

- Homicide
- Rape or sexual assault
- Aggravated assault causing serious bodily injury
- Terrorism-related conduct

Minor juvenile offenses — including vandalism, petty theft, and minor drug possession — do not trigger ineligibility and do not carry forward into aging-out assessment.

A mandatory individualized hearing is required before applying permanent ineligibility based on juvenile conduct, consistent with *Miller v. Alabama*, 567 U.S. 460 (2012), and *Roper v. Simmons*, 543 U.S. 551 (2005). A narrow rehabilitation exception is available at the individualized hearing, subject to a high evidentiary burden.

---

### Door 1 — Enhanced Voluntary Departure

#### 4.3 Eligibility

Any noncitizen without a disqualifying offense may elect enhanced voluntary departure, subject to the limitations of Sections 3A and 4.1.

#### 4.4 Requirements

- Biometric enrollment
- Identity verification
- Departure arrangements
- One-time use
- Election of Door 1 constitutes a knowing and voluntary waiver of INA §240 proceedings, preventing subsequent appeals to remain in the country on the basis of the same underlying status

#### 4.5 Outcomes

- Departure without a removal order
- Penalty waiver
- No Strike applied
- Eligible for future lawful entry
- **Priority Queue:** Door 1 electors are placed in a priority queue for certain nonimmigrant visa categories, including H-2A agricultural visas and Door 3 Worker Compliance Visas, providing a tangible and expedited path to lawful return

#### 4.6 Limitations

- Only medical-emergency waivers permitted
- Appeals limited to administrative error
- Door 1 is unavailable to individuals with pending or viable asylum claims under Section 3A
- Door 1 is unavailable to stateless persons where removal cannot be effected under Section 4.1

---

### Door 2 — Residency Track

#### 4.7 Eligibility

- 4 years continuous residence
- Up to 8 years for one minor civil violation
- Tax compliance or payment plan
- No criminal convictions beyond civil violations
- English proficiency or active enrollment in instruction
- Biometric enrollment
- Annual reporting

#### 4.8 Outcomes

- Conditional Residency
- LPR eligibility after completion
- Eligibility for citizenship per INA

#### 4.9 Limitations

- Strike 1 imposes a 5-year bar from Door 2
- Any disqualifying offense terminates eligibility

---

### Door 3 — Labor Integrity Track

#### 4.10 E-Verify National Phase-In

Amends INA §274A:

- 5-year national phase-in beginning with a pilot in 5 states
- Small-employer exemption for employers with fewer than 50 employees, subject to the aggregation rules below
- Funding per Section 9

**Anti-Fragmentation Clause**

Entities under common ownership or control as defined under 26 U.S.C. §414(b)-(c) shall be treated as a single employer for purposes of the 50-employee threshold. Common control includes parent-subsidiary relationships, brother-sister corporations, and affiliated service groups as defined under existing IRS guidance.

Restructuring occurring within 24 months prior to E-Verify phase-in triggers a rebuttable presumption of evasion, shifting the burden of proof to the employer. Penalties for proven fragmentation are treated as systemic violations under Section 4.11.

**Safe Harbor — Good Faith Compliance Certificate**

Employers who undergo a DOL-approved third-party Integrity Audit within the first 12 months of E-Verify phase-in shall receive a one-time Good Faith Compliance Certificate. The Certificate waives the rebuttable presumption of evasion for restructuring occurring prior to the audit date. Post-audit fragmentation receives no safe harbor regardless of Certificate status. The Certificate is revocable upon finding of any subsequent willful violation. Audit costs are tax-deductible to reduce barriers to participation for legitimate small businesses.

#### 4.11 Employer Penalties and Incentives

| Violation Level | Penalty |
|----------------|---------|
| First offense | $1,000 civil penalty |
| Second offense | $5,000 civil penalty |
| Systemic violation | $10,000 civil penalty |
| Willful systemic violation | Criminal penalties |

- Tax credits available for voluntary disclosure prior to audit
- Whistleblower protections for employees reporting violations

#### 4.12 Worker Compliance Visa

- Identity verification required
- English instruction required
- Tax compliance required
- Initial 3-year visa with one standard renewal (6 years maximum under standard terms)

**WCV-to-Door 2 Transition Assessment**

At year 4 of WCV status, USCIS shall conduct a mandatory assessment of the holder's eligibility for Door 2, providing a 2-year runway to cure deficiencies before the standard renewal period expires.

**Third Renewal Option**

A third renewal of up to 3 years is available contingent on demonstrated Door 2 progress, including active enrollment in English instruction, a tax compliance plan in place, and no new violations.

**Supervised Departure Option**

WCV holders who cannot meet Door 2 standards after the third renewal period are eligible for supervised departure under Door 1 terms, including penalty waivers, rather than accruing unlawful presence.

**Strike Interaction**

A WCV holder who receives a Strike 1 shall have a defined adjudication pathway and shall not automatically lose WCV status pending that adjudication.

> WCV information is not usable for enforcement purposes except in cases of fraud, national security concern, or disqualifying offenses.

---

### Door 3 — Labor Bridge Provision

#### 4.13 Strike 1 Conversion to Door 3

Strike 1 recipients currently employed by an E-Verify compliant employer at the time the Strike is triggered are eligible for conversion to Door 3 status in lieu of immediate departure, subject to the following conditions:

- Employer must be actively E-Verify compliant at the time of the Strike
- Worker must have no violations beyond the triggering Strike
- Conversion counts as the one standard WCV renewal; the worker does not receive a fresh clock
- The 5-year Door 2 bar under Section 5.1 remains in full effect; the Labor Bridge is not an amnesty provision

**Wind-Down WCV — Sectoral Staggering**

Where DHS determines that mass simultaneous Strike 1 departures in a specific sector would cause critical supply chain disruption, DHS may trigger a Sectoral Staggering mechanism. Affected workers are issued a Wind-Down WCV expiring in phases (e.g., 25% every 6 months), ensuring the law's intent — transition or departure — is eventually met while preventing acute economic shock. DHS must publish a Sectoral Staggering declaration in the Federal Register with supporting economic findings. Staggering authority is time-limited and may not be used to indefinitely defer compliance.

---

### Door 4 — National Service Track

#### 4.14 Eligibility

- No disqualifying offenses
- English proficiency
- Conditional pre-service status under amended 10 U.S.C. §504
- 2,000 military slots per year
- 5,000 civilian service slots per year

#### 4.15 Requirements

- 4 years active duty or civilian service
- 4 years inactive reserve (military track)
- Good standing throughout service period

#### 4.16 Outcomes

- Conditional Residency
- LPR eligibility
- Fast-track naturalization
- Status revocable for misconduct or fraud

---

## Section 5. Two-Strike System

### 5.1 Strike 1

Triggered by first post-enactment unlawful entry or overstay.

**Consequences:**

- Eligibility for voluntary departure or INA §240 proceedings
- No removal order without INA §240 adjudication
- 5-year bar from Doors 2-4
- Full appeal rights
- Labor Bridge conversion available for qualifying employed individuals under Section 4.13

### 5.2 Strike 2

Triggered by second post-enactment violation.

**Consequences:**

- Permanent ineligibility for all Doors
- Permanent re-entry bar
- Full due-process hearing required before permanent bar applies
- Waiver available only for extreme humanitarian cases

### 5.3 Asylum and Minor Interaction

- Strikes are tolled during the pendency of bona fide asylum claims consistent with Section 3A.2
- Strikes do not apply to unaccompanied minors consistent with Section 4.2.1

---

## Section 6. Retroactivity and Transition

- Strikes apply only to post-enactment conduct
- 36-month transition period funded per Section 9

**Capacity Benchmarking**

The 36-month transition period is tied to immigration court capacity benchmarks established by the Executive Office for Immigration Review (EOIR) within 90 days of enactment. If court staffing and caseload capacity benchmarks are not met, the transition period shall extend automatically in 6-month increments until benchmarks are satisfied, preventing a compliance cliff absent adjudicative capacity.

---

## Section 7. Sunset and Long-Term Operation

After 15 years from the date of enactment:

1. Door 1 expires
2. Doors 2-4 become permanent
3. Strike system continues indefinitely

A 6-month grace period applies for individuals with pending Door 1 applications at the time of sunset.

---

## Section 8. Oversight, Appeals, and Agency Penalties

### 8.1 Immigration Integrity Inspector General

Annual reports shall include:

- Systemic violations by enforcement personnel
- Enforcement fairness metrics disaggregated by demographics
- Border and labor enforcement metrics
- Door utilization and processing time data

Mandatory periodic GAO audits with enforceable corrective deadlines shall supplement annual IG reporting.

### 8.2 Appeals

- 30-day window to file appeals for strikes, disqualifications, or pathway denials
- **Forum:** Appeals heard by EOIR immigration courts in the first instance, with further appeal to the Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA), and thereafter to the appropriate U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals consistent with INA §242
- **Standard of Review:** De novo review of legal determinations; substantial evidence standard for factual findings
- **Timeline:** EOIR shall issue a decision within 90 days of a timely-filed appeal; failure to meet this deadline entitles the applicant to automatic administrative review by the BIA

### 8.3 Agency Penalties

- Mandatory corrective action for substantiated violations
- Up to 10% funding reduction for agencies with systemic violations
- Administrative compensation for individuals adversely affected by substantiated agency violations

## Section 9. Fiscal Provisions

**Funding Sources:**

- Civil and criminal penalties collected under this Act
- Application and processing fees established via regulation
- DHS and DOL budget reallocations
- Capped appropriations not to exceed **$15-$30 billion over 10 years**
- No unlimited mandatory spending authorized

**Immigration Court Expansion**

A dedicated appropriation for immigration court expansion is established within the EOIR budget, separate from DHS/DOL reallocations, calibrated to projected caseload generated by Four Doors intake. This appropriation shall fund surge hiring of immigration judges, asylum officers, and administrative law judges, with a staffing needs assessment required within 90 days of enactment.

**IIDPA Adjudication Office**

A dedicated IIDPA Adjudication Office shall be established within USCIS to process Four Doors pathway applications, offloading volume from immigration courts during the transition period.

**Door 1 Surge Capacity**

Dedicated funding for Door 1 processing infrastructure, including biometric enrollment capacity, departure logistics, and coordination with high-volume receiving countries, to ensure processing speed sufficient to maintain the deterrence value of voluntary departure.

**Bilateral Return Protocols**

The Secretary of State shall negotiate Expedited Return Protocols with high-volume sending nations within 18 months of enactment. Nations cooperating on orderly Door 1 returns shall be eligible to receive a portion of penalty fees collected under Section 4.11 to assist with reintegration programs, converting Door 1 from a unilateral U.S. action into a diplomatic instrument with financial incentives for cooperation.

**Interim Implementation Budget Trigger**

Core operational components — biometric enrollment, compliance window administration, and court surge staffing — are funded on a priority basis from penalty revenues and existing reallocations pending CBO score completion. Components dependent on new appropriations shall sunset automatically if appropriations are not enacted within 24 months.

**CBO Score**

A Congressional Budget Office cost estimate is required within 120 days of enactment.

## Section 10. Severability

If any provision of this Act, or the application thereof to any person or circumstance, is held invalid, the remainder of the Act and the application of its provisions to other persons or circumstances shall not be affected.

---

## Section 11. Effective Date

This Act takes effect **180 days after the date of enactment.**

DHS and USCIS shall issue implementing regulations within the same 180-day period.

---

## Section 12. Border Security & Humanitarian Provisions

### 12.1 Border Resources

$5 billion appropriated for:

- Fixed sensors, drones, and aerial surveillance systems
- Border Patrol hiring, retention, and training
- Integrated cross-agency data systems

### 12.2 Family Unity Waivers

Case-by-case humanitarian waivers available for Doors 2-4 applicants demonstrating:

- U.S.-citizen dependent hardship
- Life-threatening medical hardship

### 12.3 Border Metrics

Annual public reporting on:

- Border encounter volume and trends
- Immigration court backlogs
- Unlawful-presence population estimates

---

## Section 13. Rule of Construction

- Nothing in this Act creates new rights beyond those guaranteed by existing constitutional law
- Nothing in this Act shall be construed to limit or modify rights guaranteed by the Fifth Amendment, the Fourth Amendment, or INA §240
- DHS and USCIS shall issue implementing regulations within 180 days of enactment consistent with the Administrative Procedure Act

---

> **Pre-Enactment Consultation Required:** Prior to final enactment, the sponsoring committee shall consult with the Department of Justice, Department of Defense, Department of Homeland Security, Department of Labor, Office of Management and Budget, and relevant Congressional committees to identify statutory conflicts and ensure operational readiness.
